DEBRIEFING:


What is this study about?

  • This study examines how easily people detect speech errors when listening to people speak.

  • When we listen to conversations, we often anticipate what someone is going to say next.

Imagine predicting the word 'movies' given the two different sentences below:

1a. John has strong opinions about television shows but even more so about _movies_
1b. John likes _movies_

  • It is more difficult to accurately predict words with less context (1b).

  • With more context, people can better anticipate upcoming words (1a).



How is the study conducted?

In this task, you listened to a story from beginning to end. The speaker often made mistakes while reading (in fact, there were 60 mistakes!).

Some of those words were very predictable given their preceding sentence (like 1a), while others were less predictable (like 1b). Specifically, we had the speaker replace some determiners (e.g. the, an, a) with prepositions (e.g. of, in, with, to) and vice versa.


For example, "The books went all the way up to OF ceiling."

We are interested in whether having a strong expectation for a particular word allows people to better detect errors in sentences like (1a) rather than (1b)!



A few more questions:

Did you find yourself accidentally hitting the space bar often? Did you find the task easy or difficult?



Overall, did you notice anything about how the errors sounded? How well do you think you did?
(There were 60 mistakes to be found and most people only hear ~5-10!)
